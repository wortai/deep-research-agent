## Query
What are the specific architectural differences between ViTs and CNNs that lead to their different strengths and weaknesses in image classification?

## Solution
#### What architectural differences between Vision Transformers (ViTs) and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) cause their varying strengths and weaknesses in image classification?
Vision Transformers (ViTs) and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) differ architecturally in how they process images. ViTs divide images into patches, treating them as tokens similar to NLP, and use self-attention mechanisms to model relationships between these patches, enabling the capture of long-range dependencies. This approach provides flexibility in capturing global relationships within images. CNNs, on the other hand, utilize convolutional layers to extract local features like edges, textures, and patterns by scanning small regions of an image (receptive fields). CNNs have a strong inductive bias toward local features, which makes them easier to train on smaller datasets and ensures robust performance in many computer vision tasks. Consequently, ViTs perform better on larger datasets due to their ability to learn relationships directly from the data and minimal inductive bias, while CNNs excel in tasks requiring local feature extraction and perform better on smaller datasets due to their reliance on local features and fewer learnable parameters. ViTs tend to overfit on small datasets without pre-training or data augmentation. CNNs are also computationally more efficient than ViTs.

## Query
What are the typical performance metrics (e.g., accuracy, computational cost, data requirements) of ViTs and CNNs on standard image classification datasets (e.g., ImageNet)?

## Solution
#### How do the performance metrics such as accuracy, computational cost, and data requirements of Vision Transformers (ViTs) compare to those of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) on standard image classification datasets like ImageNet?
Vision Transformers (ViTs) have achieved state-of-the-art results in image classification tasks, particularly when trained on large datasets like ImageNet. ViTs require large datasets to perform well and avoid overfitting. They have minimal inductive bias and rely on learning relationships between patches directly from the data, making them more flexible but harder to train on smaller datasets. ViTs tend to overfit on small datasets unless pre-trained on large datasets or combined with data augmentation techniques.

CNNs generally perform better on small datasets due to their reliance on local features and fewer learnable parameters compared to ViTs. CNNs are computationally more efficient than ViTs for most tasks, as convolutions are optimized for image data. CNNs also generalize better on smaller datasets due to their built-in inductive biases toward local features such as edges and textures, which makes them easier to train and ensures robust performance.

One of the provided sources mentions ViT achieving a best accuracy of 88.4% and CNNs achieving best accuracies of 74%, 93%, 88%, and 86% on DermaMNIST, BloodMNIST, PneumoniaMNIST, and OrganCMNIST datasets, respectively. Note, however, that these are low-resolution medical image datasets, not ImageNet.
