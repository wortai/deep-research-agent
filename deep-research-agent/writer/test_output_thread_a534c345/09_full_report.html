2. Foundational Investment Principles and Performance Benchmarks

2.1 Key Performance and Risk Metrics for Investment Evaluation

To evaluate the performance of investment strategies effectively, several key metrics are essential. These metrics help investors understand both the return potential and the risk associated with various investment options. The following metrics are critical in assessing the viability of an investment:

  • CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate): This metric provides the annualized return of an investment, accounting for compounding. It is a useful measure for comparing the performance of different investments over time. CAGR classifications are as follows:
    • Below 10%: Underperforming most index funds.
    • 10-25%: Solid, typical of hedge fund performance.
    • 25-50%: Exceptional, difficult to sustain long-term.
    • Above 50%: Potentially suspicious, requires verification for overfitting or short test periods.
  • Total Return: This is the cumulative gain over the entire test period. While CAGR is better for comparisons across different time periods, total return gives a complete picture of performance over the life of the investment.
  • Maximum Drawdown (Max DD): This represents the largest peak-to-trough decline in the equity curve, indicating the worst potential loss an investor would have to endure. The classifications for maximum drawdown are:
    • Above -20%: Excellent risk management.
    • -20% to -40%: Good, typical for trend-following strategies.
    • -40% to -60%: Significant, requires consideration of one's risk tolerance.
    • Below -60%: Dangerous, potentially worse than Buy & Hold for volatile assets.
  • Sharpe Ratio: This metric measures risk-adjusted returns, calculated as (Strategy Return – Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation of Returns. It indicates the return per unit of risk. The Sharpe ratio classifications are:
    • Below 0.5: Poor risk-adjusted returns.
    • 0.5-1.0: Acceptable.
    • 1.0-2.0: Good, targeted by most successful hedge funds.
    • Above 2.0: Exceptional but requires verification.

The Sharpe ratio is a critical measure of investment performance relative to a risk-free asset, adjusted for risk. It is calculated as the difference between investment returns and risk-free return, divided by the standard deviation of investment returns. This ratio represents the additional return an investor gets for each unit of increased risk. A higher Sharpe ratio suggests better returns for the same risk level, which is generally more appealing to investors. However, it is important to note that financial assets are often not normally distributed, meaning standard deviation doesn't capture all risk aspects. A reliable Sharpe ratio estimate needs return data collected over a sufficient period to observe all aspects of the strategy returns Wikipedia - Sharpe Ratio.

The economic value of TLH is most pronounced over longer investment horizons, where the deferral of tax liabilities can be significantly leveraged.

The mechanics of TLH involve identifying underperforming assets, selling them to realize losses, and reinvesting in similar assets to maintain portfolio balance. This process is particularly effective in deferring tax obligations, especially when the portfolio is held for extended periods, allowing for the compounding of tax-loss benefits. The longer the investment horizon, the more pronounced the tax-deferral benefits of TLH become, particularly when the portfolio is not liquidated, allowing for the deferral of tax liabilities.
