1. General — Climate Change Overview

1.1 Overview

Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns. Although natural climate variations have occurred throughout Earth's history, the current warming trend is unequivocally driven by human activities since the Industrial Revolution.IPCC AR6, 2023NASA Global Climate Change. At its core, this phenomenon is amplified by the enhanced greenhouse effect, a natural process where certain gases in Earth's atmosphere trap heat from the sun. Without the greenhouse effect, Earth's average surface temperature would be approximately -18°C rather than the current +15°C. Human activities, however, have intensified this effect by substantially increasing the atmospheric concentrations of greenhouse gases (GHGs), leading to unprecedented warming and associated disruptions.

The following sections provide a comprehensive primer on the scientific causes rooted in GHG emissions, the empirical evidence from observational records, the cascading effects across ecosystems, economies, and human societies, and viable mitigation and adaptation strategies. This overview synthesizes key metrics and trends to underscore the empirical foundation of climate change as a human-induced crisis demanding urgent action.

Figure 1 illustrates the greenhouse effect process: shortwave solar radiation penetrates the atmosphere, is absorbed by the surface, and re-emitted as longwave infrared radiation, which GHGs trap, elevating surface temperatures. Human emissions have amplified this natural mechanism, elevating GHG concentrations to levels unseen in at least 800,000 years.IPCC AR6.

The primary greenhouse gases contributing to this enhancement are carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), nitrous oxide (N₂O), and fluorinated gases, responsible for 76%, 16%, 6%, and 2% of total GHG emissions, respectively.NASA CO₂, the dominant gas, originates mainly from fossil fuel combustion (coal, oil, natural gas), deforestation, and cement production; its atmospheric concentration has surged from a pre-industrial level of 280 parts per million (ppm) to 424 ppm in 2024. Methane, 28 times more potent than CO₂ over a 100-year global warming potential (GWP), stems from agriculture (particularly livestock), landfills, and natural gas production. Nitrous oxide, with a 265x GWP, arises from fertilizer use and industrial processes, while fluorinated gases—thousands of times more potent—are emitted from refrigerants and electronics manufacturing.

Empirical Evidence of Climate Change

Robust observational data confirms the human-driven warming signal across multiple indicators. Sea levels have risen by approximately 20 cm since 1900, with rates accelerating from a 2006-2015 average of 3.6 mm/year to 4.5 mm/year currently.IPCC AR6 Oceans, absorbing 90% of excess heat, reached record heat content in the upper 2,000 meters in 2023. Ice sheets are losing mass at alarming rates: Greenland at ~279 billion tons/year and Antarctica at ~148 billion tons/year. Glacier retreat affects 90% of glaciers worldwide, with the Alps losing 60% of ice volume since 1900. Ocean acidification has reduced pH by 0.1 units (a 30% acidity increase) since the Industrial Revolution. Extreme weather—heat waves, droughts, heavy precipitation, and intense hurricanes—has increased in frequency and intensity.NOAA

Effects on Ecosystems

Ecosystems face profound disruptions. Coral reefs project 70-90% loss at 1.5°C warming and >99% at 2°C, with bleaching events increasing 5-fold since the 1980s.IPCC AR6 Synthesis Biodiversity loss threatens ~1 million species, many within decades; climate ranks as the third-largest driver after land/sea use change and exploitation. Species ranges shift poleward at 17 km/decade. In the Arctic, sea ice has declined 13% per decade since 1979, shrinking polar bear habitats and triggering permafrost thaw that releases stored carbon in a positive feedback loop.WMO

Economic and Human Societal Impacts

Economic damages from extreme weather reached $313 billion globally in 2022, up from $50 billion in 1990. Projections at 2°C warming forecast 10-23% global GDP reduction by 2100 (Swiss Re); the Stern Review (2006) pegged inaction costs at 5-20% annual GDP versus 1% for mitigation.IPCC Insurance losses from climate disasters have surged 7-fold since the 1970s. Human health suffers: heat-related deaths rose 68% from 2000-2004 to 2017-2021 (WHO); vector-borne diseases like malaria and dengue expand into new areas; air pollution worsened by climate contributes to 7 million premature deaths yearly; crop yields for wheat, rice, and maize face 10-25% declines at 2°C, imperiling food security for billions.WHO

Critical Consensus: Unmitigated warming risks cascading economic contraction, with damages compounding via feedbacks like permafrost thaw, far exceeding mitigation costs by orders of magnitude.

Mitigation and Adaptation Strategies

Mitigation reduces emissions through energy transition (solar/wind now 14% of electricity, up from 2% in 2010), electrification (EVs, heat pumps), efficiency (insulation, LEDs), carbon capture (DAC, CCS, reforestation), and policies like carbon pricing (covering 23% of emissions) and trading systems. Adaptation includes infrastructure (sea walls, resilient crops), early warnings (only 50% of countries adequate), and ecosystem restoration (mangroves, green spaces).IPCC Synthesis

Key Mitigation Progress: Renewables' rapid scaling demonstrates feasibility; combined with policy, pathways exist to limit warming to 1.5-2°C.

The Paris Agreement (2015, 196 parties) targets well below 2°C, ideally 1.5°C. Yet current NDCs trajectory yields 2.5-2.9°C by 2100, underscoring the gap between science and action.WMO
