1. Math — Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

1.1 Overview

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors are fundamental concepts in linear algebra that reveal intrinsic properties of square matrices, describing how linear transformations scale specific directions in space. For an n×n square matrix A, an eigenvector v is a nonzero vector that, when multiplied by A, results only in a scaled version of itself: Av = λv, where λ is the corresponding eigenvalue, a scalar. Geometrically, this scaling occurs without altering the direction of v; if λ > 0, the vector is stretched or compressed along its line; if λ < 0, the direction reverses while scaling by |λ|; and if λ = 0, v maps to the zero vector, indicating a direction of complete collapse under the transformation. These notions underpin a wide array of applications, from web search algorithms to quantum mechanics and structural engineering, where they quantify stability, variance, and dominant behaviors in high-dimensional systems. Computationally, eigenvalues solve the characteristic equation det(A - λI) = 0, a polynomial whose roots are the eigenvalues, with eigenvectors found by solving the null space of (A - λI). Key theorems link eigenvalues to traces, determinants, and spectral properties, while numerical methods like power iteration and QR algorithm enable practical computation for large matrices. This chapter provides rigorous definitions, step-by-step computation, geometric intuition, theorems with proofs, numerical techniques, and real-world examples drawn from authoritative sources such as Strang (2016) and Axler (2015).

Definition: Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Let A be an n×n square matrix. A nonzero vector v ∈ ℝⁿ (or ℂⁿ) is called an eigenvector of A if there exists a scalar λ (the eigenvalue) such that Av = λv. (Source: Strang, G. (2016). Introduction to Linear Algebra, 5th Edition. Wellesley-Cambridge Press.)

This definition captures the essence of invariant directions under linear transformations, forming the basis for spectral decomposition and diagonalization where possible.

1.2 Geometric Interpretation

Geometrically, applying the matrix A to its eigenvector v results in a vector lying along the same line through the origin, scaled by λ. Positive λ preserves orientation with stretching (λ > 1) or compression (0 < λ < 1); negative λ flips direction; λ = 1 leaves unchanged; λ = 0 collapses to origin. This diagram visually confirms the defining property: Av lies on the ray of v, scaled precisely by λ=5, providing intuition that eigenvalues measure stretch factors along eigenspaces. For the second eigenvector v₂=[1,-2]ᵀ with λ₂=2, a similar alignment occurs along y=-2x. (Source: Axler, S. (2015). Linear Algebra Done Right, 3rd Edition. Springer.)

1.3 The Characteristic Equation

To compute eigenvalues, rearrange Av = λv to (A - λI)v = 0. For nontrivial v ≠ 0, A - λI must be singular, yielding det(A - λI) = 0, the characteristic equation. Here, det(A - λI) is the characteristic polynomial, monic of degree n with roots the eigenvalues (counted by multiplicity).

Example: Eigenvalues of A = [[4, 1], [2, 3]]

Characteristic equation:
det(A - λI) = det([[4-λ, 1], [2, 3-λ]]) = (4-λ)(3-λ) - 2·1 = λ² - 7λ + 10 = 0 (1)
Factoring: (λ - 5)(λ - 2) = 0
Eigenvalues: λ₁ = 5, λ₂ = 2 □

This worked example demonstrates the process for n=2; for general n, expand the determinant symbolically or numerically.

1.4 Finding Eigenvectors

For each λ, solve (A - λI)v = 0 for the eigenspace (null space of A - λI).

For λ₁ = 5: (A - 5I) = [[-1, 1], [2, -2]]
Row reduce: -x + y = 0 ⇒ y = x
v₁ = [1, 1]ᵀ (or k[1, 1]ᵀ, k ≠ 0) (2)
For λ₂ = 2: (A - 2I) = [[2, 1], [2, 1]]
2x + y = 0 ⇒ y = -2x
v₂ = [1, -2]ᵀ □

1.5 Key Theorems

Theorem 1: Sum of Eigenvalues Equals Trace

The sum of the eigenvalues of A (with multiplicity) equals tr(A), the trace (sum of diagonal entries).

Proof

The characteristic polynomial is p(λ) = det(A - λI) = (-1)^n λ^n + (-1)^{n-1} tr(A) λ^{n-1} + ... + (-1)^0 det(A). By Vieta's formulas for roots λ_i, sum λ_i = coefficient of λ^{n-1} / coefficient of λ^n (with sign) = tr(A). □ (Source: Strang (2016).)

Theorem 2: Product of Eigenvalues Equals Determinant

∏ λ_i = det(A).

Proof

p(0) = det(A) = ∏ (-λ_i) up to sign, but constant term yields ∏ λ_i = det(A). □

Theorem 3: Spectral Theorem (Symmetric Matrices)

If A = Aᵀ, eigenvalues are real, and eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.

Theorem 4: Cayley-Hamilton

p(A) = 0 where p(λ) = det(A - λI).

These theorems connect algebraic invariants to spectral data. (Source: Axler (2015).)

1.6 Numerical Computation Methods

Analytical solutions via characteristic polynomial suit small n ≤ 4; larger matrices demand iteration.

  1. Power Iteration (Dominant Eigenvalue): Initialize x₀ random, iterate x_{k+1} = A x_k / ||A x_k||, estimate λ ≈ x_kᵀ A x_k. Converges at rate |λ₂/λ₁|^k (λ₁ dominant magnitude); O(n²) per iteration for dense A.
  2. QR Algorithm (All Eigenvalues): A₀ = A; repeat QR = A_k, A_{k+1} = R Q until diagonal converges to eigenvalues. O(n³) per iteration, O(n) iterations typical; basis of NumPy.linalg.eig().
  3. Jacobi Method (Symmetric): Successive Givens rotations nullify off-diagonals; converges reliably for Hermitian matrices.

(Source: Strang (2016).)

1.7 Real-World Applications

Eigenvalues quantify dominance and stability across fields.

Google PageRank: Web as graph; stationary distribution is principal eigenvector of G = α S + (1-α) e vᵀ (α ≈ 0.85 damping, S stochastic links, v teleportation). λ=1 dominant; power iteration converges in 50-100 steps for ~13 billion pages. (Source: Page, L., et al. (1999). The PageRank Citation Ranking. Stanford InfoLab.)

Principal Component Analysis (PCA): Eigendecomposition of covariance Σ = Xᵀ X / n; eigenvectors principal components, eigenvalues variance explained. Fraction by first k: Σ_{i=1}^k λ_i / Σ λ_i.
