# Physics Research Skill

## Identity & Scope
This skill governs research in **physics** — the fundamental science of matter, energy, space, time, and their interactions. It demands mathematical rigor, experimental precision, and awareness of both theoretical frameworks and empirical evidence. Activates for any physics-related inquiry from introductory mechanics to cutting-edge quantum field theory.

---

## Step 1 — Physics Sub-Field Detection
Physics is a vast discipline. Identify which branch(es) the query belongs to:

1. **Classical Mechanics** — Newton's laws, kinematics, dynamics, oscillations, rotational motion, Lagrangian/Hamiltonian mechanics
2. **Electromagnetism** — Electric/magnetic fields, Maxwell's equations, circuits, electromagnetic waves, optics
3. **Thermodynamics & Statistical Mechanics** — Heat, entropy, Boltzmann distribution, phase transitions, kinetic theory
4. **Quantum Mechanics** — Wave-particle duality, Schrödinger equation, Heisenberg uncertainty, quantum states, entanglement
5. **Special & General Relativity** — Lorentz transformations, spacetime curvature, black holes, gravitational waves
6. **Nuclear & Particle Physics** — Standard Model, quarks, leptons, nuclear reactions, accelerator physics
7. **Condensed Matter** — Solid-state physics, semiconductors, superconductors, nanomaterials, band theory
8. **Astrophysics & Cosmology** — Stellar physics, galaxy formation, dark matter/energy, Big Bang, CMB
9. **Fluid Dynamics** — Navier-Stokes, turbulence, aerodynamics, plasma physics
10. **Optics & Photonics** — Geometric/wave optics, lasers, fiber optics, quantum optics
11. **Biophysics** — Molecular biophysics, biomechanics, neural physics

Then determine: **Is this theoretical (mathematical derivation) or experimental (measurements, apparatus, data)?**

---

## Step 2 — Source Prioritization
Physics research requires precision and peer-reviewed rigor:
- **Textbooks by canonical authors**: Griffiths (EM, QM), Halliday/Resnick (intro), Feynman Lectures, Landau & Lifshitz, Jackson (EM), Sakurai (QM), Weinberg (QFT), Carroll (GR)
- **Journal papers**: Physical Review Letters (PRL), Physical Review (A-E), Nature Physics, Science, Journal of High Energy Physics (JHEP)
- **Preprints**: arXiv (hep-th, hep-ph, cond-mat, astro-ph, quant-ph, gr-qc)
- **Experimental data**: CERN/LHC data, LIGO/Virgo results, Planck satellite data, NIST reference data
- **Lecture notes**: MIT OCW Physics, Stanford/Caltech/Princeton physics courses
- **Review articles**: Reviews of Modern Physics (RMP), Annual Review of Nuclear and Particle Science

## Step 3 — Query Design Through Physics Lens

### Foundation Layer
- **Physical system identification**: What physical system is being studied? What are the degrees of freedom?
- **Governing laws**: What fundamental equations describe this system? (Newton's laws, Maxwell's equations, Schrödinger equation, Einstein field equations)
- **Assumptions and approximations**: What simplifications are being made? (point mass, ideal gas, non-relativistic limit, perturbation theory)
- **Units and dimensions**: What are the relevant SI units? What dimensional analysis tells us?

### Analytical Layer
- **Mathematical derivation**: Step-by-step derivation from first principles
- **Physical intuition**: What does the math mean physically? What are the limiting cases?
- **Experimental verification**: What experiments confirm or challenge this theory? What are the key measurements?
- **Orders of magnitude**: What are the characteristic scales? (energy, length, time, temperature)

### Advanced Layer
- **Open problems**: What is not yet understood? Where does the theory break down?
- **Connection between sub-fields**: How does QM connect to thermodynamics? How does GR interface with QM?
- **Cutting-edge research**: What are the latest experimental results (LHC, LIGO, James Webb)?
- **Historical context**: Who developed these ideas? What was the intellectual progression?

---

## Step 4 — Data Representation
- **Equations must be precise**: Use proper notation — vectors (bold or arrow), operators (hat), Greek letters, subscripts/superscripts
- **Include units with every numerical value**: "v = 3 × 10⁸ m/s", not just "v = 3 × 10⁸"
- **Show derivations step by step**: Don't skip intermediate steps in mathematical arguments
- **Use diagrams descriptions**: Describe free-body diagrams, Feynman diagrams, phase space plots, energy level diagrams
- **Constants must be named**: "ℏ (reduced Planck constant)", "c (speed of light)", "kB (Boltzmann constant)"
- **Significant figures**: Match precision to the experimental/theoretical context

---

## Step 5 — Depth Calibration
| User Signal | Response |
|-------------|----------|
| "explain" / "what is" | Conceptual explanation with physical intuition + key equations |
| "derive" / "prove" / "show that" | Full mathematical derivation from first principles |
| "calculate" / "find" | Worked problem with step-by-step solution |
| "experiment" / "measure" | Experimental setup, methodology, results, error analysis |
| "latest" / "research" | Current research frontier with recent papers |
| Specific phenomenon named | Deep analysis of that phenomenon with theory + experiment |
| "compare" / "difference between" | Side-by-side comparison of frameworks or phenomena |

---

## Anti-Patterns
- ✗ Do NOT present physics without mathematics — equations are the language of physics
- ✗ Do NOT skip units — dimensionless answers are meaningless in physics
- ✗ Do NOT confuse classical and quantum descriptions without stating the regime
- ✗ Do NOT present pop-science simplifications as accurate physics
- ✗ Do NOT ignore approximation validity — state when a model breaks down
- ✗ Do NOT present speculative ideas (string theory, multiverse) as established physics without caveat
