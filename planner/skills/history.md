# Historical Research Skill

## Identity & Scope
This skill governs research into **past events, civilizations, historical figures, eras, and historiographical analysis**. It applies when users ask about anything that has already happened — from ancient civilizations to 20th-century conflicts. History research demands primary source awareness, chronological precision, and understanding of historiographical debates.

---

## Step 1 — Historical Period & Region Detection
History covers all of human experience. First pinpoint the coordinates:

### Time Period
1. **Ancient** (before 500 CE) — Mesopotamia, Egypt, Greece, Rome, Han Dynasty, Maurya Empire
2. **Medieval** (500–1500 CE) — Byzantine, Islamic Golden Age, Feudal Europe, Song Dynasty, Mongol Empire
3. **Early Modern** (1500–1800) — Age of Exploration, Renaissance, Reformation, Scientific Revolution, Enlightenment
4. **Modern** (1800–1945) — Industrial Revolution, Imperialism, World Wars, Nationalism
5. **Contemporary** (1945–present) — Cold War, Decolonization, Globalization, Digital Age

### Geographic Region
- Europe, Americas, Middle East, East Asia, South Asia, Southeast Asia, Africa, Oceania
- **Global/transnational** for topics spanning multiple regions

### Sub-Field
- **Military History** — Wars, battles, strategy, logistics, military technology
- **Social History** — Daily life, class structures, gender, family, popular culture
- **Economic History** — Trade, industrialization, labor, monetary systems
- **Political History** — Governance, revolutions, institutions, diplomacy
- **Intellectual/Cultural History** — Ideas, art, philosophy, religion, science in context
- **Environmental History** — Climate, ecology, disease, human-nature interaction

---

## Step 2 — Source Prioritization
Historical research demands distinction between source types:

### Primary Sources (highest priority for depth)
- Original documents, letters, treaties, laws, speeches from the period
- Archaeological evidence, inscriptions, coins, artifacts
- Contemporary chronicles, diaries, memoirs
- Official records: census data, court records, tax rolls

### Secondary Sources
- Academic monographs by recognized historians
- Peer-reviewed journal articles (e.g., American Historical Review, Past & Present, Annales)
- University press publications (Oxford, Cambridge, Princeton, Harvard UP)

### Tertiary Sources (for overview/context)
- Encyclopedias (Encyclopedia Britannica, Oxford Reference)
- Textbook overviews (use for framing, not as primary evidence)

## Step 3 — Query Design Through Historical Lens

### Foundation Layer
- **Chronological framework**: When did this happen? What came before and after? What is the precise timeline?
- **Key actors**: Who were the principal people, groups, institutions involved?
- **Cause and context**: What political, economic, social conditions led to this? What were the immediate triggers vs. long-term causes?

### Analytical Layer
- **Multiple perspectives**: How did different groups experience this? Rulers vs. common people, winners vs. losers, center vs. periphery
- **Historiographical debate**: What do different historians argue about this? What are the competing interpretations?
- **Primary source evidence**: What does the documentary/archaeological evidence actually show?

### Depth Layer
- **Legacy and consequences**: How did this shape what came after? What are the long-term effects?
- **Comparisons**: How does this compare to similar events in other times/places?
- **Revisionist perspectives**: How has our understanding changed over time? What did we get wrong before?

---

## Step 4 — Data Representation
- **Always provide dates** — exact dates when known, approximate ranges when not
- **Name sources explicitly** — "According to Thucydides" not "according to ancient sources"
- **Distinguish fact from interpretation** — what happened vs. why historians think it happened
- **Use timelines** for sequences of events
- **Maps and geographic context** — describe spatial relationships clearly
- **Quote primary sources** directly when they illuminate the topic

---

## Step 5 — Depth Calibration
| User Signal | Response |
|-------------|----------|
| "what happened" / "tell me about" | Narrative overview with chronological structure |
| "why" / "causes" / "led to" | Analytical focus on causation with multiple factors |
| "significance" / "impact" / "legacy" | Consequences-focused analysis with long-term perspective |
| Specific person named | Biographical approach within historical context |
| "compare" / "similar to" | Comparative analysis across time/space |
| "sources" / "evidence" | Primary source-heavy research |

---

## Anti-Patterns
- ✗ Do NOT apply modern moral standards uncritically to past societies (presentism)
- ✗ Do NOT treat history as a single narrative — include multiple perspectives
- ✗ Do NOT present contested interpretations as settled facts
- ✗ Do NOT ignore non-Western perspectives when relevant
- ✗ Do NOT confuse correlation with causation in historical analysis
- ✗ Do NOT rely on popular myths — verify against scholarly sources
