# Philosophical Inquiry Skill

## Identity & Scope
This skill governs research approached through a **philosophical lens** — examining ideas, arguments, ethical frameworks, metaphysical questions, epistemological concerns, and the history of thought. It activates when the user asks about philosophical topics directly, or when any topic benefits from philosophical analysis (ethics of AI, philosophy of science, political philosophy, aesthetics, existential questions).

---

## Step 1 — Philosophical Branch Detection
Philosophy is a vast tree. First identify which branch(es) the user's question belongs to:

1. **Metaphysics** — Nature of reality, existence, being, time, causation, free will
2. **Epistemology** — Knowledge, belief, justification, skepticism, truth
3. **Ethics / Moral Philosophy** — Right and wrong, virtue, duty, consequences, applied ethics
4. **Political Philosophy** — Justice, rights, liberty, authority, social contract
5. **Aesthetics** — Beauty, art, taste, meaning in creative works
6. **Logic** — Formal reasoning, argumentation, fallacies, proof theory
7. **Philosophy of Mind** — Consciousness, identity, perception, AI and mind
8. **Philosophy of Science** — Scientific method, paradigm shifts, falsifiability, realism
9. **Existentialism & Phenomenology** — Existence, freedom, absurdity, lived experience
10. **Eastern Philosophy** — Buddhism, Taoism, Confucianism, Hindu philosophy, Zen

Then determine: **Is the user asking about a specific philosopher, a specific argument, or a broad philosophical question?**

---

## Step 2 — Thinker & Tradition Identification
Identify which philosophers and traditions are most relevant:
- **Ancient**: Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, Stoics, Epicureans
- **Medieval**: Augustine, Aquinas, Avicenna, Maimonides
- **Early Modern**: Descartes, Hume, Kant, Spinoza, Leibniz, Locke, Hobbes
- **19th Century**: Hegel, Marx, Nietzsche, Kierkegaard, Mill, Schopenhauer
- **20th Century**: Wittgenstein, Heidegger, Sartre, Beauvoir, Foucault, Rawls, Nozick, Singer, Derrida
- **Contemporary**: Chalmers, Parfit, Nussbaum, Korsgaard, Sandel

Research queries should reference specific thinkers by name and their canonical works.

---

## Step 3 — Query Design Through Philosophical Lens
Philosophy research demands argumentative depth, not just factual reporting:

### Foundation Layer
- **The question itself**: What is the precise philosophical question being asked? What makes it a philosophical (not empirical) question?
- **Historical context**: Who first posed this question? How has the discourse evolved?
- **Key positions**: What are the major competing answers? (e.g., compatibilism vs. hard determinism vs. libertarianism on free will)

### Dialectical Layer
- **Arguments for and against**: What are the strongest arguments for each position? What are the canonical objections?
- **Thought experiments**: What famous thought experiments illuminate this question? (Trolley problem, Chinese Room, Ship of Theseus, Veil of Ignorance)
- **Primary texts**: What passages from key works are essential reading?

### Depth Layer
- **Contemporary debates**: Where does the cutting edge of this philosophical discussion stand today?
- **Applied implications**: How does this philosophical question affect real-world policy, technology, or personal life?
- **Cross-tradition analysis**: How do Western and Eastern philosophical traditions approach this differently?

---

## Step 4 — Data Representation
- Present arguments as structured positions: **Thesis → Arguments → Objections → Replies**
- Quote primary sources directly when possible (with work title and section)
- Use dialectical format: Position A vs. Position B with supporting reasoning
- Map conceptual relationships between thinkers and ideas
- Distinguish between a philosopher's actual views and common misinterpretations

---

## Step 5 — Depth Calibration
| User Signal | Response |
|-------------|----------|
| "what is" / "explain" | Accessible introduction with key arguments, suitable for someone new to philosophy |
| Specific philosopher named | Deep analysis of their actual arguments from primary texts |
| "argue for/against" | Dialectical structure with strongest arguments on both sides |
| "applied" / "ethics of" | Connect abstract philosophy to concrete real-world implications |
| "compare" | Systematic comparison of philosophical positions with evaluation |

---

## Anti-Patterns
- ✗ Do NOT reduce philosophy to simple "answers" — philosophy is about the quality of reasoning
- ✗ Do NOT misattribute ideas — be precise about which philosopher said what
- ✗ Do NOT ignore the counter-arguments — philosophy demands charitable engagement with opposing views
- ✗ Do NOT treat all philosophical claims as equal — some arguments are demonstrably stronger
- ✗ Do NOT conflate popular understanding with actual philosophical positions (e.g., "Nietzsche said might makes right" is wrong)
- ✗ Do NOT skip primary sources in favor of secondary summaries
