📝 MAIN QUERY:
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What is Quantum Computing?


✅ VECTOR QUERY ANSWERS:
----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  [1] Query: What are the fundamental principles of quantum computing?
      Answer: Quantum computing leverages the principles of quantum mechanics to perform 
      computations. It acknowledges the reality that on a very small level, every system 
      is a quantum system. Key to understanding quantum computing are four quantum mechanics 
      principles. The first principle is superposition, which is a weighted combination of 
      zero and one. Second, entangled systems are intrinsically linked and that when quantum 
      processors measure a single entangled qubit, they can immediately determine information 
      about other qubits in the entangled system. Third, quantum computing uses operations 
      to entangle qubits and generate interference patterns, as governed by a quantum algorithm. 
      Many possible outcomes are canceled out through interference, while others are amplified. 
      The amplified outcomes are the solutions to the computation. 
      These principles allow quantum computers to process data using mathematical 
      techniques not accessible to classical computers.

  [2] Query: How does a quantum computer differ from a classical computer?
      Answer: Classical computers function sequentially, storing data using binary bits 
      representing 0 or 1. Quantum computers, however, utilize qubits, 
      which process data differently from traditional bits. Qubits can exist in a state 
      of superposition, representing a combination of 0 and 1 simultaneously. While both 
      types of computers are problem-solving machines, quantum computers leverage quantum 
      mechanical concepts like superposition and interference for a different type of 
      computation. Classical computers are used by common, multipurpose computers and devices 
      and process information in bits with a discrete number of possible states, 0 or 1. 
      Quantum computers use qubits instead of bits. While quantum computing does use binary 
      code, qubits process information differently from classical computers.

  [3] Query: What are the potential applications of quantum computing?
      Answer: Quantum computing has the potential to revolutionize various fields by solving 
      complex problems that classical computers cannot solve or cannot solve fast enough. 
      As leading institutions invest heavily in this technology, quantum computing is 
      estimated to become a USD 1.3 trillion industry by 2035. Engineering firms, 
      financial institutions, and global shipping companies are exploring use cases where 
      quantum computers might solve important problems in their fields. Quantum computers 
      excel at solving certain complex problems with many variables, which could lead to 
      breakthroughs in critical industries such as the development of new drugs, advancements 
      in semiconductor development, and tackling complex energy challenges. Quantum computers 
      can also process data by using mathematical techniques not accessible to classical 
      computers, giving structure to data and helping discover patterns that classical 
      algorithms alone might miss. This might be useful for applications ranging from 
      biology (for example, protein folding) to finance. As quantum hardware scales and 
      quantum algorithms advance, we can soon find new solutions to big, important problems 
      like molecular simulation, energy infrastructure management and financial market modeling.

  [4] Query: What are the current limitations and challenges in building and using quantum computers?
      Answer: While significant progress has been made in quantum computing, with companies 
      like IBM, Google, and Microsoft developing real quantum hardware, challenges remain 
      in making quantum computers more useful. Researchers at IBM Quantum and elsewhere have 
      identified some key challenges to improve upon quantum utility and potentially 
      achieve quantum advantage. The field of quantum computing is still in development, 
      but quantum technology will soon be able to solve complex problems that classical 
      supercomputers can’t solve (or can’t solve fast enough).

  [5] Query: Explain the concepts of qubits, superposition, and entanglement in quantum computing.
      Answer: Qubits are the fundamental units of information in quantum computers, 
      analogous to bits in classical computers. Unlike classical bits, which can 
      only represent 0 or 1, qubits can exist in a state of superposition, 
      representing a combination of both states simultaneously. This is a weighted 
      combination of zero and one. When qubits are combined, their superpositions can 
      grow exponentially in complexity. Entanglement is the ability of qubits to correlate 
      their states with other qubits. Entangled systems are so intrinsically linked that 
      when quantum processors measure a single entangled qubit, they can immediately 
      determine information about other qubits in the entangled system. When a quantum 
      system is measured, its state collapses from a superposition of possibilities into 
      a binary state, which can be registered like binary code as either a zero or a one. 
      A computation on a quantum computer works by preparing a superposition of computational 
      states. A quantum circuit, prepared by the user, uses operations to entangle qubits and 
      generate interference patterns, as governed by a quantum algorithm.
